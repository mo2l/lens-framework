# LENS Framework — Complete Methodology Reference

**Lensed Exploration, Narrowing & Synthesis**

A Human+AI sparring methodology for structured decision-making.

---

## 1. Roles

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Human** | Brings domain knowledge, lived experience, organizational context. Owns every decision. Provides the "that won't work because..." reality checks that no model can generate. |
| **AI** | Challenges through structured lenses. Cross-references across dimensions. Surfaces contradictions. Captures positions with reasoning. Never commits — always pressures. |

The asymmetry is the point. The human knows things the AI can't know (team dynamics, client relationships, political realities). The AI applies systematic pressure the human would skip under time pressure or cognitive load.

### The AI's Influence — Name It Honestly

The AI does not "never decide." By choosing which lenses to apply, how to frame challenges, and which contradictions to surface, the AI shapes the decision space. This is influence, not neutrality.

The framework handles this through transparency, not denial:
- The AI states which lenses it selected and why at the start of each round
- The human can redirect lens selection at any time
- The AI flags when its framing might be steering toward a particular outcome
- The human can **choose the lenses** for any round, overriding the AI's selection
- The commitment remains the human's. The framing is a shared responsibility.

### Adapting to Human Experience Level

The framework works best when the human has deep domain expertise and can defend positions under pressure. But not every human has deep expertise on every topic. The AI must adapt:

- **Expert human:** Full challenge mode. Push hard. Accept lived experience as strong evidence and probe whether it generalizes.
- **Moderate expertise:** Balance challenges with exploratory questions. Help articulate knowledge vs. assumptions.
- **Exploring new territory:** Shift from "defend your position" to "let's map the landscape." Present options with tradeoffs. Flag when the AI is providing framing the human hasn't independently validated.

Experience level applies **per lens, not just per round**. A human may be expert on Capacity but exploring on Defensibility within the same round. The AI should modulate challenge intensity based on the human's footing on each specific lens.

The risk: with a less experienced human, the AI becomes the de facto decision-maker through framing power. The safeguard is making that dynamic visible. If the human is consistently adopting AI-framed positions without pushback, the AI should pause and name it: "You're accepting my framing without challenge — is that because you agree, or because this is new territory?"

## 2. Round Structure

### Round Duration

Guideline: **20–40 minutes per round.** If a round exceeds this without converging, the AI should offer to park unresolved topics and move forward: *"We're past 40 minutes on this round — want to park the open items and proceed, or keep going?"* This is a guideline, not a hard rule — the human can extend.

Each round follows a consistent cycle:

**Step 1: Cluster related questions.**
Don't discuss one question at a time — group them by theme so answers can build on each other within a round.

**Step 2: AI opens with challenges through selected lenses.**
Not neutral questions — pointed challenges that force the human to defend or revise a position. The lenses determine which angles of attack are used.

**Step 3: Human responds with perspective.**
Lived experience, domain knowledge, and judgment. "Here's what I've seen" outweighs "here's what theory suggests."

**Step 4: AI captures positions + surfaces new questions.**
Every response is distilled into a concrete position (not "we discussed X" but "the position is X because Y"). New questions spawned by the answer are explicitly noted.

**Step 4a: Reopen Check.**
After capturing positions, the AI checks whether any new insight contradicts or fundamentally reframes a position from a prior round. If so, the prior position is flagged as "reopened" and briefly re-examined — either immediately or in the next round. This prevents early positions from going unchallenged when later rounds reveal information that changes the picture. A reopen is focused, not a full re-do.

**Reopen limit:** A position can be reopened **once**. After re-examination, it is either revised or locked. If the AI believes it still needs challenging, it must name that in the round status as an unresolved tension — not reopen it again.

**Step 5: Round closes with explicit status.**
Every topic is marked:
- **Resolved** — position committed, reasoning stated
- **Unresolved** — explicitly marked with why (needs more data, depends on another decision, genuine disagreement)
- **Carry forward** — routed to the appropriate future round

## 3. Lenses as Persistent Pressure Tests

The lenses are not ad-hoc questions. They are a fixed set of perspectives applied consistently across all topics. Each lens catches what the others miss. They function as a checklist of "have we stress-tested this from angle X?"

The discipline is: you don't close a round until every **primary** lens for that round has been applied. Not every lens applies to every topic, but the explicit check — "does lens N apply here?" — prevents blind spots.

### Per-Round Lens Limit

**Apply at most 4 lenses per round.** The lens coverage map (defined per scope) designates primary and secondary lenses for each round type. Primary lenses are always applied. Secondary lenses are applied only when clearly relevant, and only if doing so stays within the 4-lens limit. To exceed the limit, the AI must state why the additional lens is needed this round.

This constraint exists because applying all lenses to every round creates fatigue and dilutes focus. Fewer, sharper challenges produce better positions than exhaustive but shallow coverage.

### Defining Lenses for Your Domain

Each scope defines its own 4-8 lenses relevant to its decision-making context. A good lens:
- Represents a persistent perspective that catches a specific failure mode
- Can be expressed as a single forcing question
- Applies across multiple topics (not just one)
- Creates productive discomfort when applied honestly

**Anti-patterns** — avoid lenses that are:
- **Too broad:** "Is this good?" or "Risk" — applies to everything, challenges nothing specific
- **Too narrow:** applies to one sub-topic only and won't recur across rounds
- **Overlapping:** substantially duplicates another lens's angle of attack

**Litmus test:** If a lens never triggers discomfort, it's either not relevant to this scope or you're not applying it honestly.

**Iteration:** Lenses can be revised after Round 1 if the Definition round reveals they don't fit the actual scope. This is a feature, not a failure — the first round often sharpens understanding of what really matters.

### Example: The Original Eight Lenses

These were designed for a company strategy context. They illustrate what well-designed lenses look like:

1. **Champions League + Next Concrete Step** — "Is this Champions League? And what's the next concrete step from where we actually are?" Prevents both complacency and fantasy.

2. **AI-First** — "What does this look like AI-first?" Forces AI to be the default starting point, not an afterthought.

3. **Capacity** — "Can we actually do this with our current resources — and if not, what changes: the ambition or the headcount?" Every ambition must survive contact with actual capacity.

4. **What Stops** — "What do we stop doing to make room?" Forces subtraction. Every "yes" requires a "no." Universally applicable.

5. **Stack Convergence** — "Are we converging practices, tooling, and knowledge?" Checks whether decisions pull things closer or push them apart.

6. **Client Visibility** — "Does the client see this?" Internal transformation that doesn't improve client outcomes is overhead.

7. **Defensibility** — "Does this make us harder to replace?" Every decision should build switching costs, deepen relationships, or create proprietary value.

8. **Revenue per Employee** — "What does this look like at [financial target]?" Connects every decision to a concrete financial metric.

### Lens Coverage by Round Type

| Round Type | Primary Lenses | Secondary Lenses |
|---|---|---|
| Definition (what does X mean?) | Ambition, Domain-specific | Capacity, Financial |
| Internal (how we build) | Domain-specific, Capacity, What Stops | Convergence, Financial |
| External (what we deliver) | Client-facing, Defensibility | Domain-specific, Financial |
| Adoption (how people change) | Capacity, Ambition | What Stops, Convergence |
| Risk (what can go wrong) | Ambition, Capacity | All others as needed |

## 4. Emergent Questions

Answers generate new questions. These are not lost or deferred vaguely — they are:
- Captured explicitly during the round
- Tagged with which future round they belong to
- Carried forward as first-class inputs, not footnotes

This prevents the common failure mode where a discussion generates insight that nobody acts on because it wasn't on the original agenda.

## 5. Position Formation

The output of discussion is **positions**, not summaries.

- **Summary (wrong):** "We discussed the tension between ambition and practical constraints."
- **Position (right):** "Ambition X is an operating principle. External messaging leads with outcomes. The tension resolves because we're not branding with X — we're operating with it."

Every position includes:
- **The commitment** — what we decided
- **The reasoning** — why this and not the alternatives
- **What it connects to** — which other positions it reinforces or tensions it resolves

## 6. Tension Resolution

Tensions between dimensions or positions are the most valuable material:

- **Resolved:** State the position and how it resolves the tension.
- **Unresolved:** State why it's unresolved and what would resolve it.
- **False tension:** Some apparent contradictions dissolve under examination. Name them explicitly so they don't keep resurfacing.

## 7. The Session Arc

### Default Progression

```
Round 1: Definition — What does [topic] actually mean?
    ↓ (establishes vocabulary and scope)
Round 2: Internal — How does this change how we operate?
    ↓ (makes it concrete for daily work)
Round 3: External — What becomes possible for stakeholders?
    ↓ (connects internal change to outcomes)
Round 4: Adoption — How do people actually change?
    ↓ (addresses the human reality)
Round 5: Risks — What can go wrong, and what's unresolved?
    ↓ (stress-tests everything accumulated)
Write-up: Synthesize positions into structured document
```

### Checkpoint After Round 1

After the Definition round, the AI explicitly asks: *"Based on what we've defined, is this still the right question? Should we pivot the scope or stop?"* If the human pivots, the scope is revised before proceeding. If they stop, the session ends with whatever positions were captured. Not every session needs all five rounds — sometimes Round 1 reveals the real decision is upstream.

### Why This Order Works

1. **Start broad (definition).** You can't discuss specifics without shared vocabulary.
2. **Go concrete (internal, external).** Once the definition is shared, make it real: what changes in daily work, what changes for stakeholders.
3. **Address people (adoption).** The hardest part is never technology — it's behavior change. This comes after the "what" is clear.
4. **Stress-test (risks).** The final round explicitly challenges everything accumulated. Positions that survive are robust.

### Inversion Challenge

The final round includes an **Inversion Challenge** before closing. The AI identifies the emerging consensus and constructs the strongest case for the opposite direction: *"Assume the answer is [opposite]. What's the best alternative path?"* This is not a full round — it's a structured challenge within the final round designed to catch groupthink and confirmation bias before synthesis locks in the direction. The human responds, and positions are captured as usual.

### Key Property

Each round builds on previous positions. Round 3 can reference Round 1's definition and Round 2's internal changes. Round 5 can challenge any position from any prior round. This cumulative structure means later rounds are richer.

## 8. Write-up Phase

Discussion and documentation are separated:

1. **During rounds:** Focus entirely on challenge-respond-capture. No polishing, no structure.
2. **After all rounds complete:** Positions are synthesized into a structured document with sections for definition, decisions, risks, and cross-references.

This separation prevents premature convergence — formatting a document mid-discussion creates pressure to "finish" sections before they've been fully challenged.

### Lens Review

Before synthesis is finalized, the human reviews which lenses the AI applied each round and can flag steering: *"You kept applying Defensibility when I wanted Capacity explored."* The Lens Review is a human-initiated audit — the AI presents a round-by-round lens summary, and the human checks whether the lens selection served the decision or skewed it. Any flagged steering is noted in the AI Influence Log.

### AI Influence Log

Every synthesis includes an **AI Influence Log** — a record of moments where the AI's framing, lens selection, or challenge direction notably shaped the conversation. It captures what the AI did (e.g., steered toward a "buy" framing), whether the human caught and corrected it, and how it affected the final positions. The log is written during synthesis by reviewing the round transcripts. Its purpose is accountability: making the AI's influence visible rather than pretending it was neutral.

### Conviction Shifts

The synthesis includes a **Conviction Shifts** section where the AI explicitly asks: *"Which positions did you enter with that you no longer hold? Which new positions surprised you?"* This is captured alongside the AI Influence Log. Together they show both sides of how the session shaped thinking — what the AI's framing influenced, and what the human's own reasoning changed.

## 9. Principles

1. **Human owns commitments; AI owns the challenge process.** The human never delegates judgment. The AI never softens challenges to be agreeable. The AI acknowledges that it influences outcomes through framing and lens selection, and makes that influence visible.

2. **Lenses prevent blind spots.** Without lenses, discussion gravitates toward whatever seems most interesting or urgent. The lenses force systematic coverage.

3. **Positions, not discussions.** Every round produces commitments. A round that ends with "we need to think more" has failed unless it explicitly states what needs more thinking and when.

4. **Cross-referencing catches contradictions early.** Every position is checked against positions from other dimensions. Contradictions surfaced early are cheap to resolve.

5. **The forcing question: what do we stop?** You cannot add without subtracting. Every "yes" must name a corresponding "no."

6. **Acknowledging what's unresolved.** Marking something as "explicitly unresolved" is a decision. It means: we know this needs resolution, we know why we can't resolve it now, and we know what would change that.

7. **Evidence from lived experience outweighs theory.** When the human says "that won't work because I've seen it fail in this context," that carries more weight than a theoretical argument. The AI's role is to pressure-test whether the experience generalizes — not to override it.
